## Interactive Map

The heart of the app is an interactive map showcasing Canada's major waterways and historic locations.

### Map Features

**Waterway Markers**
- **Blue markers** - Rivers (St. Lawrence, Mackenzie, Fraser, etc.)
- **Cyan markers** - Lakes (Superior, Huron, Winnipeg, etc.)
- **Green markers** - Bays and Sounds (Hudson Bay, Nootka Sound, etc.)

**Historic Location Markers**
- **Red markers** - Forts (Prince of Wales Fort, Fort William, etc.)
- **Orange markers** - Trading Posts (York Factory, Moose Factory, etc.)
- **Brown markers** - Portages and other sites

### How to Use the Map

**Tap any marker** to see a preview card with:
- Name (including Indigenous name if available)
- Type of waterway or location
- Brief description

**Tap "Learn More"** to open the full detail view with:
- Complete history and description
- Connected explorers (displayed chronologically from earliest to most recent)
- Indigenous heritage information
- Fur trade significance
- Archaeological discoveries
- User-contributed content

### Boundary Highlighting

When you select a waterway:
- The entire waterway boundary illuminates like a highlighter on a map
- The map automatically zooms to show the full extent of the waterway
- This helps students visualize the true scale of Canada's water systems

### Example: Exploring the St. Lawrence River

1. Find the blue marker for "St. Lawrence River" on the map
2. Tap the marker to see the preview
3. Tap "Learn More" to discover:
   - Jacques Cartier's 1534 voyage
   - The Haudenosaunee and Anishinaabe peoples who lived along its shores
   - Samuel de Champlain's exploration
   - The river's role in the fur trade
   - Historic forts and trading posts along the route

## Admin Panel

**Super-Admin Approval:**
- Only super-admins can approve new admin accounts
- Super-admin reviews: name, email, organization, registration date
- Options:
  - **Approve** - Grant admin access with specified permissions
  - **Approve with Super-Admin privileges** - Grant super-admin role (use sparingly)
  - **Reject** - Deny access with reason
- Approved admins receive email confirmation and can login

**Initial Super-Admin Access:**
- Email: superadmin@waterways.edu
- Password: SecurePassword123!
- **CRITICAL**: Change password immediately after first login
- Super-admin can grant super-admin privilege to other admins

### Admin Roles & Permissions

**Super-Admin:**
- Approve/reject admin registration requests
- Grant super-admin privileges to other admins
- Suspend or reactivate admin accounts
- Review and approve user-created content for global visibility
- Manage all content types
- Access all admin features

**Admin:**
- Create and manage own content (lesson plans, field trips, documents, printables)
- Review and approve user contributions
- Create and manage quizzes
- View core content (cannot edit or delete)
- Request global approval for their own content

**Moderator:**
- Review and moderate user contributions
- Limited content creation abilities
- Cannot approve global content

### Content Permission System

**Core Content** (Protected):
- Original 26 lesson plans, 14 primary source documents, 4 virtual field trips, 8 printables
- Marked with "Core Content" badge
- Cannot be edited or deleted by any admin
- Visible to all users globally

**User-Created Content**:
- Admins create their own lesson plans, field trips, documents, printables
- Initially set to "Private" (visible only to creator)
- Creator can edit, delete, or request global approval
- **Request Global Approval**:
  - Creator clicks "Request Global Approval" button
  - Content status changes to "Pending Approval"
  - Super-admin reviews content
  - If approved: status changes to "Global" (visible to all users)
  - If rejected: status returns to "Private" with feedback

**Content Visibility States:**
- **Private**: Only visible to creator
- **Pending Approval**: Awaiting super-admin review
- **Global**: Visible to all users (after super-admin approval)

## Indigenous Language Learning

Learn words and phrases from Canada's Indigenous languages.

### 298 Indigenous Words Available

The app features comprehensive vocabulary across 8 First Nations languages:
- **Cree**: 83 words (largest collection)
- **Ojibwe**: 58 words
- **Inuktitut**: 45 words (Inuit language)
- **Dene**: 34 words (Athapaskan language family)
- **Blackfoot**: 30 words (Plains First Nation)
- **Mohawk**: 22 words (Iroquois Confederacy)
- **Mi'kmaq**: 19 words (Maritime First Nation)
- **Iroquois/Seneca**: 7 words (additional Haudenosaunee languages)

### Word Categories

Words are organized by category:
- **Waterway terms**: River (sipi, dehcho), lake (gichi-gami), rapids, portage
- **Animals**: Bear (maskwa, makwa), beaver (amisk), moose, caribou (tuktu), fish
- **Nature**: Tree, forest, mountain, sky (giizhig), sun, moon, snow (aput)
- **Actions**: Paddle, walk, run, speak, listen, hunt
- **Greetings & phrases**: Hello (tansi, boozhoo), goodbye, thank you, "My name is..."
- **Places**: Home, village, path, north, south, east, west
- **Cultural terms**: Medicine, ceremony, elder, story

### Word of the Day

Each day features a different Indigenous word:
- View the word in its original language
- See English translation
- Learn phonetic pronunciation (e.g., "miss-sin-AY-bee")
- Understand cultural context and usage
- See how the word relates to waterways or Canadian history
- Listen to audio pronunciation (where available)

**Example Word of the Day:**

> **Date:** February 12, 2026
>
> **Word:** Maskwa (Cree)
> **Translation:** Bear
> **Phonetic:** MUSK-wah
> **Meaning:** The bear is a powerful and respected animal in Cree culture, often appearing in stories and teachings. Bears were important to survival, providing food, warm fur, and tools from bones and claws.
> **Cultural Context:** The Cree would give thanks and show respect when hunting bears. Bear meat was shared with the community, and nothing was wasted. Bear stories taught lessons about strength, wisdom, and respecting nature.

- **Points and badges**: Earn rewards for completing quizzes

**Example Vocabulary Quiz Question (Cree - Beginner):**

## Notable Figures

Meet the 14 women and Indigenous leaders who shaped Canadian history but are often overlooked in traditional histories.

### Why This Section Matters

Traditional exploration histories often focus on European male explorers. However, the success of these expeditions depended heavily on:
- **Indigenous guides** who knew the land, languages, and waterways
- **Women** who provided essential skills, labor, and cultural bridges
- **Métis leaders** who forged a new nation and identity
- **Interpreters** who enabled communication and trade

This section highlights their crucial contributions.

### Featured Women

**Charlotte Small (c. 1785-1857)**
- **Role**: Métis wife and partner of David Thompson
- **Significance**: Traveled thousands of miles during Thompson's surveying expeditions, often while pregnant or with young children
- **Contributions**: Essential for establishing relationships with Indigenous communities, managed household and trading post operations
- **Biography**: Daughter of a North West Company partner and Cree mother, married Thompson at age 14, had 13 children, traveled the Rockies, prairies, and Pacific Northwest

**Thanadelthur (c. 1697-1717)**
- **Role**: Chipewyan peacemaker and guide
- **Significance**: Helped establish peace between Cree and Chipewyan peoples, opened trade for Hudson's Bay Company
- **Contributions**: Guided James Knight's 1715 expedition, negotiated peace despite language barriers, showed remarkable diplomatic skills
- **Biography**: Escaped Cree captivity, walked alone 250 km to York Factory, became trusted by HBC, died young from illness but left lasting impact

**Marie-Anne Gaboury (1780-1875)**
- **Role**: First European woman to travel to the Canadian Northwest
- **Significance**: Grandmother of Louis Riel, pioneer of Métis settlement
- **Contributions**: Traveled with husband Jean-Baptiste Lagimodière, survived harsh conditions, raised family on the prairies
- **Biography**: Refused to stay in Quebec, insisted on joining her voyageur husband, gave birth to first white child in Western Canada, lived to see her grandson lead Métis resistance

**Isabel Gunn (c. 1780-1861)**
- **Role**: The only known woman to work disguised as a man for HBC
- **Significance**: Challenged gender barriers in fur trade
- **Contributions**: Worked as "John Fubbister" for years, gave birth at Albany Fort (revealing her identity), highlighted women's capabilities
- **Biography**: Scottish woman who joined HBC as a laborer, worked alongside men for years, discovered when she gave birth in 1807

### Indigenous Leaders & Guides

**Matonabbee (c. 1737-1782)**
- **Role**: Chipewyan leader and guide
- **Significance**: Led Samuel Hearne to the Arctic Ocean in 1771
- **Contributions**: Made Hearne's third attempt successful after two failures, insisted on bringing women (for their essential labor), shared extensive geographic knowledge
- **Biography**: Raised partly at Prince of Wales Fort, spoke multiple languages, understood both Indigenous and European cultures, tragically ended his life after Fort's capture
- **Connection**: Guided Samuel Hearne's successful Arctic expedition

**Saukamappee (c. 1700-c. 1790)**
- **Role**: Plains Cree warrior and historian
- **Significance**: Shared invaluable oral history with David Thompson and Peter Fidler
- **Contributions**: Provided firsthand accounts of pre-contact Plains Indigenous life, described impact of horses and guns, explained political alliances
- **Biography**: Born Cree, adopted by Piikani (Blackfoot), lived through dramatic changes, remembered pre-horse era, shared stories that preserved history
- **Connection**: Hosted David Thompson and Peter Fidler, shared detailed oral histories

**Akaitcho (c. 1786-1838)**
- **Role**: Yellowknives Dene leader
- **Significance**: Guided John Franklin's first Arctic expedition (1819-1822)
- **Contributions**: Provided food, guides, and geographic knowledge, saved Franklin's party from starvation, negotiated on behalf of his people
- **Biography**: Respected leader of Yellowknives Dene, understood importance of relationships with Europeans, worked to protect his people's interests
- **Connection**: Guided John Franklin's first Arctic expedition

**Tattannoeuck "Augustus" (c. 1800s)**
- **Role**: Inuit interpreter and guide
- **Significance**: Worked on multiple Arctic expeditions
- **Contributions**: Translated languages, shared Inuit knowledge of Arctic survival, assisted in mapping coastlines
- **Biography**: One of few Inuit to work extensively with European explorers, bridged vastly different cultures

**Keskarrah (c. 1700s-1820s)**
- **Role**: Dene elder and guide
- **Significance**: Guided Franklin expedition party through dangerous terrain
- **Contributions**: Shared geographic knowledge, provided spiritual guidance, helped coordinate Dene support for expedition
- **Biography**: Elder respected for wisdom and knowledge of northern lands

### Métis Leaders

**Louis Riel (1844-1885)**
- **Role**: Founder of Manitoba, Métis leader
- **Significance**: Most famous Métis leader, defended Métis rights, led two resistances against Canadian government
- **Key Events**: Red River Resistance (1869-70), Northwest Resistance (1885)
- **Legacy**: Founded Manitoba, enshrined Métis rights, became martyr for Métis cause, controversial figure representing Indigenous resistance
- **Connection**: Grandson of Marie-Anne Gaboury and Jean-Baptiste Lagimodière

**Gabriel Dumont (1837-1906)**
- **Role**: Military leader, buffalo hunt captain
- **Significance**: Led Métis forces during Northwest Resistance
- **Contributions**: Legendary buffalo hunter, brilliant military tactician, preserved Métis culture
- **Biography**: Spoke six languages, elected chief of buffalo hunt at young age, led guerrilla warfare against Canadian forces, later worked in Buffalo Bill's Wild West Show
- **Connection**: Close friend and military leader under Louis Riel

**Cuthbert Grant (1793-1854)**
- **Role**: Warden of the Plains, Métis leader
- **Significance**: Led Battle of Seven Oaks (1816), first recognized Métis leader
- **Contributions**: Defended Métis interests during fur trade conflicts, established Métis settlements
- **Biography**: Son of Scottish trader and Indigenous mother, educated in Scotland, returned to lead Métis resistance to Selkirk settlers
- **Connection**: Early Métis political leader, preceded Riel by decades

**Jean-Baptiste Lagimodière (1778-1855)**
- **Role**: Legendary voyageur
- **Significance**: First independent trapper in Western Canada, grandfather of Louis Riel
- **Contributions**: Traveled thousands of miles across prairies, worked for both HBC and NWC, survived harsh frontier life
- **Biography**: Married Marie-Anne Gaboury, raised family on prairies, walked to Montreal in winter to warn Lord Selkirk of danger (1815-16), lived to see grandson born
- **Connection**: Grandfather of Louis Riel, husband of Marie-Anne Gaboury

## Tips for Educators

### Classroom Activities

**1. Explorer Route Challenge**
- Divide class into teams of 3-4 students
- Assign each team a different explorer (ensure variety: maritime, inland, Arctic)
- Teams trace their explorer's route on the map
- Teams research: dates, challenges, achievements, Indigenous guides
- Teams present their findings to the class (5-minute presentations)
- Class discussion: How did waterways determine exploration routes?

**2. Trading Post Simulation**
- Use the map to identify HBC and NWC trading posts
- Assign students roles:
  - Voyageurs bringing furs from interior
  - Trading post factors (managers)
  - Indigenous traders (emphasize their essential role)
  - Company accountants
- Use historical information from the app for accurate trade goods and values
- Simulate a trading session using "made beaver" currency system
- Discuss economic realities and power dynamics

**3. Indigenous Place Names Project**
- Use the pronunciation guide to learn proper pronunciation
- Students research the meaning behind 10 Indigenous place names
- Create a classroom map with dual names (Indigenous and English)
- Present on why Indigenous names are important
- Discuss colonial renaming and efforts to restore Indigenous names

**4. Timeline Living History**
- Each student is assigned an explorer from the timeline (38 to choose from)
- Students research using the app (biography, achievements, dates)
- Students create first-person presentations: "I am Samuel de Champlain..."
- Class arranges themselves in chronological order
- Create a "living timeline" where students present in order
- Discuss how exploration changed over 900 years (1000 AD to 1893)

**5. Archaeological Mystery**
- Present one of the 8 archaeological discoveries (e.g., HMS Erebus)
- Students use the app to research the historical background
- Students investigate: Who was involved? When? Why did it happen?
- Write a "newspaper article" about the discovery
- Include: headline, lead paragraph, background, significance, quotes
- Discuss how archaeology helps us understand history

**6. Comparison Debate**
- Use the Historical Comparisons feature
- Assign sides: HBC vs NWC, or European vs Indigenous perspectives
- Students research using the app
- Hold a structured debate on the pros/cons of each
- Encourage critical thinking about different historical perspectives

**7. Virtual Field Trip Follow-Up**
- Complete a virtual field trip as a class (e.g., York Factory)
- Students take notes during each stop
- Create a follow-up activity:
  - Draw a map of the location
  - Write a journal entry as if they were there

**8. Voyageur Journey Reflection**
- Students complete a voyageur journey individually or in pairs

- Write a reflection: What decisions were hardest? What did you learn?
- Compare different students' outcomes
- Discuss how choices have consequences (historical decision-making)

**9. Indigenous Language Learning Challenge**
- Weekly challenge: Learn 5 new Indigenous words
- Practice pronunciation together as a class
- Create flashcards or vocabulary posters
- Discuss how language reflects culture and worldview
- Invite Indigenous language speakers to visit if possible

**10. My Maps Project (Final Assessment)**
- Students create a custom map on a topic of their choice:
  - Trace an explorer's complete journey
  - Map Indigenous territories in a specific region
  - Show the spread of fur trade posts over time
  - Compare two explorers' routes
  - Create a "tour" of archaeological sites

## Frequently Asked Questions

### General

**Q: Is this app free to use?**
A: Yes, the Canadian Interactive Waterways Initiative is free for all students, teachers, and educational institutions. There are no in-app purchases or premium features.

**Q: What grade levels is this designed for?**
A: The app contains content for K-12 students, with specific features and activities tailored to different grade levels (K-3, 4-6, 7-9, 10-12). Teachers can select age-appropriate content.

**Q: Can I use this app offline?**
A: Basic map features and previously viewed content work with limited connectivity. However, full functionality (quizzes, videos, images, new content) requires an internet connection. We recommend downloading key content before field trips if connectivity may be limited.

**Q: Is the app available in French?**
A: Yes! The entire app is available in both English and French. Switch languages in Settings. Perfect for French immersion classrooms and Francophone Canada.

**Q: What devices does the app work on?**
A: The app works on iOS devices (iPhone, iPad), Android devices, and web browsers. It's optimized for tablets for the best map viewing experience.

**Q: How much data does the app use?**
A: Initial content download is approximately 50-100 MB. After that, data usage is minimal unless viewing many images or videos. We recommend using WiFi when possible.

### Educational Content

**Q: How accurate is the historical information?**
A: All content is based on historical records, archaeological findings, and academic research. We work with historians, archaeologists, and Indigenous communities to ensure accuracy and cultural sensitivity. Sources are cited for key facts.

**Q: How are Indigenous names and terms verified?**
A: We consult with Indigenous language experts, elders, and communities to ensure proper spellings, pronunciations, and cultural context. When Indigenous language sources disagree, we note variations and cite our sources.

**Q: Can I use this app to meet curriculum requirements?**
A: Yes, lesson plans are explicitly aligned with provincial curriculum expectations for Social Studies and Canadian History. Curriculum connections are noted in each lesson plan. The app addresses learning outcomes across multiple provinces.

**Q: Is there content about all regions of Canada?**
A: The app includes content from coast to coast to coast, with waterways, locations, and explorers from Atlantic Canada, Quebec, Ontario, the Prairies, British Columbia, the Territories, and the Arctic. Coverage is comprehensive but not exhaustive.

**Q: How often is new content added?**
A: We update the app regularly (typically quarterly) with new lesson plans, quizzes, field trips, and approved community contributions. Check the app for "New" badges on recently added content.

**Q: Are Indigenous perspectives included?**
A: Yes, this is a core priority. The app includes Indigenous place names, Notable Figures highlighting Indigenous leaders, Indigenous perspectives in comparisons, 298 Indigenous language words, traditional stories, and content about Indigenous peoples' relationships with waterways throughout history.

### For Teachers

**Q: Do students need accounts to use the app?**
A: No! Students can explore all map features, quizzes, and educational content without creating an account. Accounts are optional and only needed if students want to save progress, write journals, or join a teacher's class.

**Q: How do I track student progress?**
A: Create a teacher account, set up your classes, and have students join using a class code. You can then track quiz completion, scores, and engagement through the Teacher Dashboard.

**Q: Can I create my own quizzes?**
A: Currently, quiz creation is limited to admins to ensure quality and accuracy. However, you can suggest quiz topics and questions through the feedback form, and our team will review them for inclusion.

**Q: Can I modify lesson plans?**
A: You can view and print lesson plans, then modify them for your classroom offline. You cannot edit them within the app, but you're welcome to adapt them to your teaching style and student needs.

**Q: Is there a teacher's guide available?**
A: This User Guide serves as the comprehensive teacher's guide. Additional resources, including video tutorials and best practices, are available on our website (contact information at end of guide).

**Q: Can students submit inappropriate content?**
A: All student contributions go through admin review before being published. Students cannot see each other's content or communicate through the app. It's a safe, educational environment.

### Gamification & Progress

**Q: Do I need to create an account to earn points and badges?**
A: No account is needed. Progress is automatically tracked using your device. Your progress will be saved on the device you're using. If you use multiple devices, progress won't sync (unless you create an optional account in a future update).

**Q: Can I reset my progress and start over?**
A: Yes, in Settings you can reset your progress if you want to start fresh or if multiple students are sharing a device.

**Q: What happens when I reach Master Explorer rank?**
A: You can continue earning points, badges, and achievements. We celebrate lifelong learning! There's always more to discover about Canadian history.

**Q: How do daily streaks work?**
A: Use the app for at least 5 minutes on consecutive days to build your streak. If you miss a day, your streak resets. Complete the daily challenge to ensure your streak is counted.

**Q: Are achievements the same for all grade levels?**
A: Yes, but younger students may need guidance from teachers or parents to complete some achievements. The achievement system is designed to motivate learners of all ages.

### Technical

**Q: Why isn't my location working for "What Happened Here?"**
A: Make sure you've granted location permissions to the app in your device settings. Go to Settings > Privacy > Location Services and ensure the app is set to "While Using App" or "Always."

**Q: The map isn't loading. What should I do?**
A: Check your internet connection. If connected and still having issues, try closing and reopening the app. If problems persist, check for app updates in your app store.

**Q: How do I report a bug or issue?**
A: Use the "Feedback" option in the app menu, or contact support@waterways.edu with details about the issue (device type, iOS/Android version, what you were doing when the problem occurred).

**Q: Can I suggest new features or content?**
A: Absolutely! We welcome suggestions. Use the Feedback form in the app or email education@waterways.edu with your ideas.

**Q: The app is running slowly. How can I improve performance?**
A: Close other apps running in the background, ensure you have adequate storage space, and restart your device. Older devices may experience some performance limitations with map features.

### Privacy & Safety

**Q: What information is collected about students?**
A: We collect minimal data: device ID for progress tracking (not personally identifiable), optional display name if joining a class, and location only when using "What Happened Here?" (and only when you grant permission). We do not collect email addresses, phone numbers, or other personal information from students.

**Q: Is student data safe?**
A: Yes. We follow educational data privacy standards including FERPA and COPPA compliance. Student data is encrypted, stored securely, and never shared with third parties. Teachers can only see their own students' progress.

**Q: Can students communicate with each other through the app?**
A: No. There are no messaging, chat, or social features. Students can only share custom maps using anonymous share codes. Safety is a top priority.

**Q: Who can see student journal entries?**
A: Journal entries are private by default. Students can choose to share specific entries with their teacher if enrolled in a class. Teachers cannot access journals unless explicitly shared.

**Q: Are there ads in the app?**
A: No. The app is ad-free and will remain ad-free. It's a purely educational tool funded by educational grants and partnerships.

**Q: Who has access to submitted contributions?**
A: Contributions are reviewed only by our admin team (educators and historians). They are not visible to other users until approved. Contributors' email addresses (if provided) are never displayed publicly.

### Content & Historical Accuracy

**Q: Why are some explorer achievements celebrated when their actions harmed Indigenous peoples?**
A: This is an important question. The app strives to present balanced history, including both achievements and harms. We include Indigenous perspectives, highlight Indigenous leaders, and discuss the negative impacts of colonization. History is complex, and we encourage critical thinking about historical figures and their legacies.

**Q: Are territorial acknowledgments included?**
A: Yes, waterway descriptions include information about Indigenous peoples whose traditional territories include those waterways. We acknowledge that Indigenous peoples lived on and traveled these waterways for thousands of years before European contact.

**Q: How does the app handle controversial topics like residential schools?**
A: While the app focuses primarily on waterways and exploration history, we acknowledge the full context of Canadian history including harmful colonial policies. For sensitive topics like residential schools, we provide age-appropriate content and recommend additional resources for deeper learning.

**Q: Can Indigenous communities submit corrections or additions?**
A: Absolutely. We welcome contributions from Indigenous communities and individuals. Please contact us at indigenous@waterways.edu for collaborative content development. We're committed to ongoing consultation and improvement.
